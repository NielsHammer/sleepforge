In seventeen eighty-three, an English geologist named John Michell sat at his desk. He was not primarily a physicist. He studied stone, layers of earth, the patient ground beneath our feet. But he was also an amateur astronomer, a man whose mind could drift between the bedrock and the stars.

Michell composed a letter to a fellow scientist named Henry Cavendish. The letter was quiet, methodical, unrushed. In it, he posed a mathematical thought experiment. What, he wondered, if you took the laws of gravity that Newton had given us — the same laws governing falling apples and orbiting planets — and followed them toward their limit?

What if an object became so massive, so densely compressed, that the pull of gravity at its surface exceeded the speed of light itself? If you tried to escape from its grasp, you'd need to travel faster than light. But nothing travels faster than light. It's the universal speed limit, the final boundary that nothing can cross. So escape would become impossible.

Michell's imagination worked through the numbers. He calculated that a star five hundred times the radius of our sun but compressed to the same density would possess this terrible property. All the light produced within it — every photon, every ray — would be pulled back inward. The light would never reach an observer at a distance. The star would vanish. It would become invisible, permanently dark, a kind of dark star that no outside observer could ever perceive.

He did not call it sensational. He simply calculated the numbers and mailed the letter. In his time, his idea remained mostly a curiosity. A mathematical note. A quiet question with no apparent answer waiting in return.

But Michell had done something profound. He'd taken the mathematics of gravity and followed them toward a place where they seemed to strain. Where they seemed to predict something impossible — an object you could never see, an escape you could never make.

The intuition was remarkable. A geologist in England, working by candlelight, had glimpsed something true about the cosmos. But what Michell lacked was precision. He knew an object could trap light if dense enough. But he didn't know exactly how small a mass must be compressed to do so. He didn't have the exact formula. He didn't know the precise boundary.
