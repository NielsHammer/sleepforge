In nineteen sixty-five, an engineer named Gary Flandro studied the paths of distant planets. He was mapping a future no one would see. The four great worlds of the outer solar system — Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune — they'd aligned with one another. A rare configuration. One that happens only once every one hundred seventy-six years.

He'd discovered something that year. For a brief window in time, beginning in nineteen seventy-seven, a single spacecraft could use each planet's gravity as a slingshot, moving from one world to the next. A journey that'd otherwise take centuries. It would open for just a few years, then close. And wouldn't open again until the year twenty one fifty-three.

Nearly two centuries. That's how long another generation would have to wait before they could make this same attempt. The choice was settled, then, in its own quiet way. If humanity wanted to reach those distant worlds — if they wanted to know what waited there in the silence and the cold — they'd have to act now. A small window. A brief one. But it was open.

The spacecraft was ready. No one yet knew what they would find waiting at Jupiter.


Voyager 1 crosses the threshold. The spacecraft spends four months in Jupiter's realm, sending back images that astronomers will study in growing wonder.

On March eighth, nineteen seventy-nine, a navigation engineer named Linda Morabito is processing a star-background image of Io. She's calibrating the spacecraft's position when something emerges from the data. Rising from the moon's curved edge, a crescent-shaped smear climbs three hundred kilometers above the surface. It's a plume. Molten rock and sulfur gases erupting from deep beneath the frozen crust, fountaining outward into the void. The first active volcano ever discovered beyond Earth. Erupting at that very moment.

Io should've been a cold, dead world of ancient frozen rock. But it isn't. Its interior churns with heat, with geysers that stain the surface in brilliant yellows and oranges. Much remains unknown about how such a small moon can harbor such violence.

But Morabito's discovery poses a question. If this moon is hiding such a churning world beneath its icy skin, what might Saturn's system conceal? The spacecraft will arrive there in sixteen months. Something there will prove even harder to explain.


Voyager arrives at Saturn. The cameras fill with details humans have never witnessed before.

Dark spokes radiate across the B ring like wheels. They shouldn't be there. Bradford Smith's imaging team photographs them on November twelfth, nineteen eighty. The spokes rotate in lockstep with Saturn's magnetic field, not following Keplerian orbital mechanics. Inner particles move slower than physics predicted. The rules are breaking.

Much remains unexplained. Much remains hidden even now. The probe drifts toward what matters most.


That something is Titan.

Mission planners thread the probe through Titan's gravitational field on November twelfth, knowing it'll bend the path thirty-five degrees above the plane of the planets. A conscious sacrifice — Uranus and Neptune won't be visited. But Titan's thick, nitrogen-rich atmosphere, opaque at one and a half times Earth's pressure, won't come this way again. One chance only.

Ejected above the plane and accelerating at sixty-one thousand kilometers per hour, Voyager One is leaving. The solar system falls away.
