He lies down. The narrow bed receives him. His students are gathered, some weeping, some holding back. Socrates looks at them with compassion. Don't cry for me, his gaze says.

I've lived well. I've examined my life. I've done what I could to wake others from ignorance. The coldness is moving upward now. His legs are numb. But his voice remains clear. He speaks to Crito of small practical things, even as death approaches.


His breathing becomes slower. Deeper. Each breath is fully present. He's not gasping or struggling. The cold has spread throughout his body now. His chest still rises and falls.

His eyes are still open. Still clear. Still awake. This is what it means to be a philosopher—to remain awake, even to your own death. To examine it, to experience it, to not look away. The examined life extends into the examined death.


He is still. The breath has stopped. The eyes remain open but don't see. The face is peaceful. No sign of struggle. Only rest. Only the stillness of a long journey ended.

Outside, Athens continues, unaware the man who taught it to question is gone. But he isn't gone. He lives in every question. In every examined life. In every soul brave enough to ask: Am I living well?
