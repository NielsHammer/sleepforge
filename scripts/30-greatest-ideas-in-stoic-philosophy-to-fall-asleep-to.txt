Anger is temporary madness. The moment you recognize this, it loses power. You feel rage rising — at an insult, an injustice — and you can watch it. Notice it like a cloud passing. You don't have to become it. Seneca knew anger from the inside. He felt it. But he chose clarity. When anger comes, slow your breath. Close your eyes. Let the cloud pass. You're not the anger.


An emperor is chosen to lead. He doesn't choose the hardship that comes with it. Winter at the frontier, plague among the legions, battles that drain everything. He could complain. Instead, he accepts. This is what duty looks like. Not excitement. Not glory. Just acceptance. You were born. You'll die. In between, there's work. Duty is simply doing what comes before you, when it comes, without complaint. That's how to live well.


The day is over. The students have gone. He sits in the gathering dark. In accepting what he cannot change — his exile, his poverty, his suffering — he found something unshakeable. Not happiness exactly. Something deeper. A kind of rest that doesn't depend on anything external. Everything outside him is uncertain. Inside, he's quiet. You can be too. You're learning. There's nothing left to prove. Just breath.


The emperor closes his eyes. His hand rises and falls with each breath. Slower. Slower. He thinks: I will return to dust. My body. My thoughts. My empire. All dust. He's at peace with this. Not because he wants to die, but because he understands. Nothing holds on. Everything flows back into the earth. You're not separate from this. You're already dust learning to rest. Let yourself settle. Sleep now.
